## Overview
This file lists potential candidate cities for PAPO–Heatwaves simulations. 
These cities are illustrative examples chosen because they combine:

* high or increasing heat risk
*	meaningful public health or climate impacts
*	availability of open or semi-open data
*	relevance for policy-aware, opportunity-oriented simulation

This list is non-exhaustive and non-prescriptive.
Forks are encouraged to replace or extend this list with local contexts.
_____________________________________
## Selection Criteria
Cities listed here typically exhibit one or more of the following:

*	Extreme or chronic summer heat (including night-time heat)
*	Heat combined with humidity or air quality risks
*	Documented vulnerable populations
*	Existing heat action plans or emergency protocols
*	Open data portals or strong institutional data capacity
*	Clear relevance for equity-focused simulations

## North America
### Phoenix, Arizona (USA)

* Extreme, sustained heat with long heat seasons and severe night-time heat
*	National leader in heat mitigation policy (e.g., heat officers, cool corridors)
*	Strong city–county–state coordination on heat response
*	Excellent open data, dashboards, and climate–health analytics
*	Ideal for stress-testing capacity limits and timing of interventions

### Austin, Texas (USA)

*	Rapidly increasing number of extreme heat days
*	Tech-forward city culture with robust open data infrastructure
*	Strong academic–city collaboration (UT Austin)
*	Clear stress on energy grid, outdoor workers, and vulnerable populations
*	Well suited for simulations linking heat, power outages, and opportunity feasibility

### Houston, Texas (USA)

*	Extreme heat combined with high humidity (physiologically dangerous conditions)
*	Large, diverse, and socioeconomically heterogeneous population
*	Rich public health, hospital, and environmental datasets
*	Mature disaster-response infrastructure (adaptable from hurricane response to heat)
*	Strong candidate for equity and resource-allocation simulations

### Los Angeles, California (USA)

*	Heat risk compounded by air quality and urban heat islands
*	Extensive open datasets across health, environment, and infrastructure
*	Complex, multi-layer governance structure
*	Useful for simulations examining coordination friction and policy fragmentation

### Miami, Florida (USA)

*	Heat combined with humidity and broader climate vulnerability
*	High exposure among elderly populations and outdoor workers
*	Relevant for simulations linking heat preparedness with broader climate resilience

### Cary, North Carolina (USA)

*	Humid heat conditions affecting suburban populations
*	Significant elderly population and car-dependent mobility patterns
*	Housing stock variability relevant to indoor heat exposure
*	Proximity to Research Triangle Park (UNC, Duke, NC State) enables academic collaboration
*	Good candidate for small-scale, high-resolution simulations

## International
### Singapore

*	Chronic heat and humidity year-round
*	Extremely strong data infrastructure and digital public services
*	Highly integrated governance across health, transport, and housing
*	Ideal for simulations exploring tight policy–system–citizen coupling

### Paris, France

*	Heat formally recognized as a public health emergency
*	Established heat action plans and mortality tracking systems
*	Strong national–local policy integration
*	Suitable for simulations examining policy thresholds and escalation logic

### Madrid, Spain

*	Chronic and intensifying summer heat
*	National heat–health frameworks in place
*	Strong epidemiological and climate data availability
*	Useful for comparative European heat-response simulations
